Social Credit is a distributive philosophy of political economy developed by Major Clifford Hugh Douglas (1879–1952), a British engineer and economist who first articulated its core principles in *Economic Democracy* (1920) and elaborated them in *Credit-Power and Democracy* (1920) and numerous subsequent works. Social Credit holds that the fundamental cause of chronic economic depression and poverty is not insufficient production but a structural deficiency in consumer purchasing power — a gap between the total cost of production and the total wages paid to those engaged in production. This gap, Douglas argued, is not an accident of market conditions but a permanent feature of industrial accounting, remedied in practice only by consumer debt and export surpluses, which generate exponentially increasing debt loads that periodically collapse into depression.

Social Credit was particularly influential in Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and Alberta in the 1930s through 1950s, where it provided the platform for the Social Credit Party of Canada and the Social Credit Party of Alberta. It remains a minor but persistent current in heterodox economics and in the political economy of agrarian and populist movements.

## The A + B theorem

The analytical core of Social Credit is the A + B theorem, first stated by Douglas in *Credit-Power and Democracy* (1920). Douglas divided the costs incurred by any manufacturing or commercial organization into two groups:

**Group A** — all payments made to individuals: wages, salaries, and dividends.

**Group B** — all payments made to other organizations: raw materials, bank charges, and other costs.

Douglas argued that while A + B represents the total cost of production (which is reflected in the total price of goods), only A payments go directly into the purchasing power of consumers in the period under review. B payments are made to other organizations — which in turn distribute them partly as A payments and partly as their own B payments — but the key point is that B costs cannot be recovered from consumers in the period in which they arise. The effective consumer purchasing power available to buy any batch of goods is therefore less than A + B: consumers cannot, at any given time, buy back the full product of industry. The chronic deficiency must be made up either by bank loans (consumer credit), by government deficit spending, or by export surpluses — all of which create claims against future production and generate cumulative debt.

Douglas's critics — including John Maynard Keynes, who called Douglas "an army of heretics" and considered him wrong but not foolish — argued that the A + B theorem misunderstands the velocity of money and the timing of income streams: B payments do eventually become A payments through the supply chain, and the theorem fails to account for this circulation effect. Subsequent critics including Ralph Hawtrey, J.M. Pullen, and Gary North argued that Social Credit's proposed remedies would be inflationary rather than stabilizing.

## Social Credit's fourth factor of production

Douglas proposed that cultural inheritance — the accumulated knowledge, techniques, and inventions of past generations — constitutes a fourth factor of production alongside land, labour, and capital. This factor is the common inheritance of all humanity, not the private property of any individual or class. The financial profits extracted by banks and financial institutions from the creation and circulation of credit represent, in Douglas's analysis, a private appropriation of what is properly a social inheritance. The socialization of this benefit — distributing it to all members of society as a National Dividend — would restore the correct relationship between production and purchasing power.

## Policy proposals

Douglas proposed three primary policy mechanisms for implementing Social Credit:

**The National Dividend.** Every citizen would receive a periodic payment — the National Dividend — representing their share of the cultural inheritance. This dividend would be paid by the national government, not out of tax revenues, but through the creation of new credit. The dividend would be unconditional — not tied to employment, means-testing, or any other condition — and would represent the automation dividend: the income flowing from mechanized production that displaces human labour. Douglas conceived the National Dividend as what would today be called a basic income or universal basic income, though his justification was different from contemporary basic income proposals.

**The Compensated Price.** Rather than raising nominal incomes alone — which would simply translate into higher prices — Douglas proposed a Compensated Price mechanism: retailers would sell goods at a discount below cost price, and the national government would compensate retailers for the difference. The compensation rate — the "compensated price ratio" — would be set equal to the ratio of the national consumption to national production, adjusting dynamically for changes in productivity. The effect would be to reduce consumer prices to match purchasing power rather than raising incomes to match prices.

**Debt-free national credit.** Both the National Dividend and the Compensated Price would be funded not through taxation or government borrowing (which would merely recirculate existing purchasing power) but through the creation of new credit by a national credit authority — a government body issuing money directly into the economy without interest charges or debt obligations.

## Political influence and the Alberta experiment

Social Credit theory was influential in agrarian communities in Canada and Australia during the Great Depression, where farmers and small producers experienced the purchasing power deficiency acutely. The Social Credit Party of Alberta, led by William Aberhart ("Bible Bill"), won the 1935 Alberta provincial election on a Social Credit platform. The government attempted to implement Social Credit policy through "prosperity certificates" — local scrip bearing a face value of one dollar, issued to citizens as a partial National Dividend. The certificates were designed to depreciate if not spent, creating a velocity incentive.

Douglas publicly opposed the Aberhart government's implementation on the grounds that it misapplied Social Credit principles — the prosperity certificates were scrip, not national credit, and their issuance was too small and improperly structured to remedy the purchasing power deficiency. The Canadian federal government disallowed the Alberta Social Credit legislation as ultra vires in 1937, and the experiment ended without demonstrating the theory's validity. The Social Credit Party of British Columbia held provincial power from 1952 to 1991 but implemented no Social Credit monetary policy, becoming effectively a center-right non-Social-Credit government.

## Reception and legacy

Keynes characterized Social Credit as "an economic heresy" but acknowledged that it addressed a genuine deficiency that mainstream economics had failed to explain satisfactorily — the persistence of unemployment and underconsumption even during periods of high productive capacity. Later heterodox economists, including some post-Keynesians and Modern Monetary Theory proponents, have recognized partial overlaps between Social Credit analysis of money creation and their own frameworks, though the structural relationship is debated.

Social Credit as a movement is distinct from the Social Credit political parties that adopted the name without consistently implementing Social Credit policy. Douglas maintained throughout his life that no government had genuinely attempted Social Credit, and that the political movements carrying the name had been captured by interests opposed to genuine monetary reform.
