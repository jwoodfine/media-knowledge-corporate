Catholic Social Teaching (CST) is the body of doctrine developed by the Roman Catholic Church on matters of political economy, social organisation, and the proper ordering of economic life. It constitutes a sustained intellectual tradition spanning more than a century, beginning formally with the papal encyclical *Rerum Novarum* (1891) and continuing through encyclicals, council documents, and episcopal statements to the present. Catholic Social Teaching does not prescribe a specific economic system but articulates principles — human dignity, the universal destination of goods, subsidiarity, solidarity, and the priority of labour over capital — from which a range of institutional arrangements can be derived.

Catholic Social Teaching is simultaneously conservative and radical in the sense that it affirms private property as a natural right rooted in human dignity while insisting that property carries social obligations and that productive resources should be broadly distributed rather than concentrated. It is precisely this combination — affirmation of property against collectivism, insistence on its social function against laissez-faire — that made CST the intellectual source for the cooperative movement, distributism, and the social market economies of post-war Western Europe.

## The foundational documents

**Rerum Novarum (1891)** — Pope Leo XIII's encyclical on "the rights and duties of capital and labour" is the founding document of Catholic Social Teaching. It rejected both socialism, which proposed abolition of private property, and laissez-faire capitalism, which treated labour as a commodity. It affirmed the right of workers to a just wage, the right to form trade unions and cooperatives, and the duty of the state to intervene when necessary to protect the weak. Its central argument against socialism — that the solution to the condition of the working class is not the abolition of property but its wider distribution — became the foundational premise of distributism.

**Quadragesimo anno (1931)** — Pius XI's encyclical issued forty years after *Rerum Novarum* elaborated the concept of subsidiarity (that higher bodies should not absorb functions that lower bodies can perform), called for the reorganization of economic life around vocational groups or guilds rather than class conflict, and issued the most severe critique of capitalism from a papal source, arguing that concentrated economic power had corrupted not merely the market but the state itself.

**Mater et Magistra (1961)** — John XXIII extended CST to questions of international development and the obligations of wealthy nations to developing ones, and endorsed worker participation in enterprise ownership and management as a dimension of the social order — a principle that had been institutionalized in the German Mitbestimmung system and the Mondragón cooperatives.

**Laborem Exercens (1981)** — John Paul II's encyclical on the dignity of work established the "priority of labour over capital" as a central CST principle: capital is accumulated work and serves work's purposes, not the reverse. It provided the theological basis for worker cooperative structures in which capital is an instrument of the workers rather than workers being instruments of capital.

**Centesimus Annus (1991)** — John Paul II's centennial response to *Rerum Novarum*, written at the collapse of European communism, affirmed the market economy while insisting on its embedding in a just legal order and a strong cultural framework that cannot be reduced to market logic.

## Core principles

**Human dignity.** Every person possesses inalienable dignity by virtue of being human. Economic institutions are legitimate insofar as they respect and support this dignity; they are to be judged by how they affect the least advantaged members of society.

**Universal destination of goods.** The goods of the earth are intended for the use of all humanity. Private property is affirmed as a legitimate and indeed necessary institution — it is more efficient, more productive of social goods, and more consonant with human freedom than collective ownership — but property rights are not absolute. Private property carries a social mortgage: owners have obligations regarding its use, and extreme concentrations of property that exclude others from participation in economic life are incompatible with the universal destination of goods.

**Subsidiarity.** Functions that can be performed by smaller social units — the family, the local community, voluntary associations, enterprises — should not be absorbed by larger ones including the state. Subsidiarity is not anti-statism; it affirms the state's role in correcting injustice and maintaining the framework within which lower-level institutions can operate. The principle was codified in Article 5 of the Treaty on European Union.

**Solidarity.** Persons are social beings; their fulfillment is achieved through relationships with others. Solidarity is not charity but a social virtue — the recognition that we are mutually responsible for one another's wellbeing. Solidarity grounds the case for strong social institutions: trade unions, mutual benefit societies, professional associations, and cooperative enterprises.

**Priority of labour over capital.** Work is not simply a factor of production to be traded at market price; it is a human activity through which persons express and develop themselves. The priority of labour over capital means that economic institutions should be structured so that capital serves human work rather than human beings serving capital — a principle that grounds worker ownership, co-determination, and cooperative enterprise as preferred institutional forms.

**Preferential option for the poor.** Public policy and institutional design should be evaluated by how they affect the most vulnerable members of society. This principle does not mandate any specific redistribution programme but requires that the interests of the poor be the primary reference point for economic institutions.

## Institutional applications

Catholic Social Teaching is not an economic blueprint but a set of principles that have been applied in different institutional arrangements by thinkers and practitioners in the CST tradition.

The **cooperative movement** drew directly on CST's endorsement in *Rerum Novarum* of workers forming "associations" for mutual benefit — what the encyclical described as a remedy for the condition of labour superior to either socialist collectivism or laissez-faire liberalism. The Mondragón cooperative federation in the Basque Country was founded explicitly on CST by Father José María Arizmendiarrieta, who cited *Rerum Novarum* and *Quadragesimo anno* as foundational texts.

**Distributism** — the political economy of G.K. Chesterton and Hilaire Belloc — extrapolated from *Rerum Novarum*'s proposition that the solution to labour's condition is wider distribution of property, not its abolition. Distributists argued for a society of small producers, artisans, and farmers as the institutional expression of CST principles.

The **social market economy** of post-war West Germany was developed by Christian Democratic thinkers and policymakers who drew explicitly on CST — particularly its emphasis on subsidiarity, human dignity, solidarity, and the social function of property. The Freiburg ordoliberal school that provided the theoretical foundations of the social market economy incorporated CST premises about competitive order, strong intermediate institutions, and the social obligations of property.
