A cooperative is an autonomous association of persons united voluntarily to meet their common economic, social, and cultural needs and aspirations through a jointly owned and democratically controlled enterprise. The cooperative form is distinguished from other business structures by two foundational principles: member ownership of the enterprise (rather than investor ownership) and democratic governance on the basis of one member, one vote (rather than one share, one vote). These two principles mean that the cooperative enterprise is owned and governed by those who use it, rather than by those who have invested capital in it.

The International Cooperative Alliance, founded in London in 1895, defines and promotes the cooperative form globally. By 2012, approximately one billion people held membership in cooperatives across 96 countries, and the top 300 cooperative enterprises generated a combined annual turnover of approximately $2.2 trillion. Research has found that cooperatives survive their first five years of operation at a rate of approximately 80 percent, compared with approximately 41 percent for other business forms — a difference attributed to the alignment of member and enterprise interests and the absence of short-term shareholder pressure.

## The Rochdale Principles

The seven Rochdale Principles — derived from the practices of the Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers, founded in 1844 by 28 weavers — define the cooperative enterprise form and distinguish it from mutual benefit societies, employee share ownership plans, and other participatory arrangements that share some but not all of its characteristics.

**Voluntary and open membership.** Cooperatives are voluntary organizations, open to all persons able to use their services and willing to accept the responsibilities of membership, without gender, social, racial, political, or religious discrimination.

**Democratic member control.** Cooperatives are democratic organizations controlled by their members, who actively participate in setting policies and making decisions. Elected representatives are accountable to the membership. In primary cooperatives, members have equal voting rights: one member, one vote.

**Member economic participation.** Members contribute equitably to, and democratically control, the capital of the cooperative. At least part of that capital is the common property of the cooperative. Members receive limited compensation, if any, on capital subscribed as a condition of membership. Surpluses are allocated to develop the cooperative, to benefit members in proportion to their transactions with the cooperative, or to support community activities approved by the membership.

**Autonomy and independence.** Cooperatives are autonomous, self-help organizations controlled by their members. If they enter into agreements with other organizations, including governments, or raise capital from external sources, they do so on terms that ensure democratic member control and maintain cooperative autonomy.

**Education, training, and information.** Cooperatives provide education and training for their members, elected representatives, managers, and employees so they can contribute effectively to the development of the cooperative. They inform the general public about the nature and benefits of cooperation.

**Cooperation among cooperatives.** Cooperatives serve their members most effectively by working together through local, national, regional, and international structures.

**Concern for community.** Cooperatives work for the sustainable development of their communities through policies approved by their members.

## History

The cooperative form emerged in the early nineteenth century as a response to the social dislocations of the Industrial Revolution. The Fenwick Weavers' Society, established in 1761 in Ayrshire, Scotland, is frequently cited as the earliest known cooperative. Robert Owen's New Lanark model community (from 1810) demonstrated the social and economic benefits of cooperative organization at an industrial scale.

The Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers, founded 24 October 1844 with 28 weavers contributing one pound each, established the principles that would define the cooperative form globally. The Rochdale Pioneers opened a shop selling food goods at lower than market prices, paying dividends on purchases rather than on shares — a structural decision that aligned enterprise success with member use rather than capital investment. The International Cooperative Alliance was founded in London in 1895, convening cooperatives from ten nations.

Credit cooperatives — credit unions in North American usage, *caisses populaires* in Quebec, *Volksbanken* and *Raiffeisenbanken* in Germany and Austria — emerged in mid-nineteenth century Germany, driven by Hermann Schulze-Delitzsch (urban credit cooperatives, 1850s) and Friedrich Wilhelm Raiffeisen (rural credit cooperatives, 1860s). Alphonse Desjardins established the first *caisse populaire* in Quebec in 1900. As of the most recent global census, 7,950 credit unions operated in the United States with approximately 90 million members and $679 billion in deposits; the World Council of Credit Unions reported 68,882 financial cooperative institutions in 109 countries with more than 235 million members and more than $1.7 trillion in assets.

## Types

**Consumer cooperatives** purchase goods or services on behalf of their members, distributing surplus as a dividend in proportion to purchases. Retail consumer cooperatives (grocery, hardware, bookstore) follow this model.

**Worker cooperatives** are owned and governed by the employees of the enterprise. Members contribute equity capital and exercise governance rights, typically on a one member, one vote basis regardless of relative equity contribution. The Mondragón Corporation in the Basque Country is the most frequently studied worker cooperative federation.

**Housing cooperatives** own residential property and grant rights of occupancy to member shareholders. Market-rate housing cooperatives allow members to realize appreciation; limited-equity cooperatives restrict transfer prices to preserve affordability for successive members; zero-equity cooperatives are functionally equivalent to rental tenure with cooperative governance.

**Credit unions and cooperative banks** are financial cooperatives owned by their depositor and borrower members. Their cooperative structure means that surplus is returned to members as higher deposit rates, lower loan rates, or reduced fees rather than distributed to external shareholders.

**Agricultural cooperatives** — dairy, grain, fruit, wine, fisheries — allow individual producers to pool resources for processing, marketing, and distribution at scales not available to individual farm enterprises. Many of the world's largest branded food products are produced by agricultural cooperatives.

**Multi-stakeholder cooperatives** combine two or more classes of members — workers, consumers, investors, and community supporters — in a single governance structure, requiring specified combinations of classes to approve major decisions.

## The Cooperative Marque

The Cooperative Marque — a stylized design indicating the C logo — and the `.coop` internet domain (introduced 2002; `.coop` domain 2014) are reserved for organizations that meet the International Cooperative Alliance's definition of a cooperative enterprise and commit to operating by the Rochdale Principles. Both serve to distinguish genuine cooperatives from organizations that use cooperative terminology without meeting cooperative governance standards.
