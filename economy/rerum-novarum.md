*Rerum Novarum* — Latin for "Of New Things" or "Of Revolutionary Change" — is the encyclical letter issued by Pope Leo XIII on 15 May 1891. Subtitled "Rights and Duties of Capital and Labour," it is the founding document of modern Catholic social teaching: the body of doctrine concerning the proper ordering of economic and social life, the rights of workers and owners, and the duties of the state with respect to both.

*Rerum Novarum* was written in response to the social dislocations produced by the Industrial Revolution — the rapid urbanization, the emergence of a landless industrial proletariat, the growth of large-scale capital enterprise, and the spread of socialist and anarchist doctrines that proposed revolutionary alternatives to the capitalist order. Leo XIII positioned the Church between the two dominant economic frameworks of his era: the laissez-faire liberalism that treated labour as a commodity subject only to market forces, and the socialism that proposed to abolish private property entirely and vest ownership of the means of production in the state.

## The rejection of socialism

*Rerum Novarum*'s most famous argument is its refutation of the socialist remedy for the condition of labour. Leo XIII argued that private property is a natural right, grounded in human reason and in the nature of persons as free and rational beings who must plan for the future and provide for their families. The socialist claim that property should be held in common would, he argued, work against the interests of workers themselves: it would deprive the laborer of the incentive to work, dissolve the family as the primary unit of economic and social life, and transfer to the state a power over individuals that no state authority can legitimately claim.

The key passage is direct: "The first and most fundamental principle, therefore, if one would undertake to alleviate the condition of the masses, must be the inviolability of private property." The solution to the condition of labour is not the abolition of property but its wider distribution — a position that would become the foundation of distributism.

## The critique of laissez-faire liberalism

*Rerum Novarum* was equally critical of the liberal capitalist framework that treated the labour contract as a purely private agreement between employer and employee, with no legitimate role for state intervention, trade associations, or moral standards external to the market.

Leo XIII argued that labour is not a commodity but a human activity — that the worker brings not merely capacity but the person to the labour relation. A labour contract that forces workers to accept wages insufficient to sustain a decent human life is not a legitimate contract, regardless of its formal legality. The state has not only the right but the duty to intervene in the labour market to protect workers from exploitation — to set minimum conditions of safety, limit working hours for women and children, and ensure that wages are sufficient for the worker to support a family.

The encyclical explicitly endorsed the right of workers to form trade associations and cooperatives: "It is within the right of the State to intervene, when common advantage demands, to prevent one class from oppressing another and to see to it that just wages be paid to the worker." This passage provided the authoritative basis on which subsequent generations of Catholic social thinkers and labour organizers would ground their advocacy for trade unions.

## The concept of the just wage

Central to *Rerum Novarum* is the doctrine of the just wage. Leo XIII argued that the wage a worker receives must be sufficient to enable the worker to maintain himself and his family in reasonable comfort — what would today be termed a living wage. The wage set by the free labour market — what the employer is willing to pay and the worker desperate enough to accept — is not automatically a just wage.

The just wage doctrine has been elaborated by subsequent encyclicals. *Quadragesimo anno* (Pius XI, 1931) refined it to include the needs of the household, not merely the individual worker; *Laborem Exercens* (John Paul II, 1981) extended it to encompass the dignity of the working person and the priority of labour over capital.

## Subsidiarity and the role of the state

*Rerum Novarum* established the principle that came to be known as subsidiarity — that larger social bodies, including the state, should not absorb functions that smaller units (the family, the guild, the local community) can perform. This principle, later named explicitly in *Quadragesimo anno* (1931), runs throughout Leo XIII's treatment of the proper role of the state in economic life: the state should intervene to protect the weak, maintain justice, and ensure minimum conditions for human flourishing, but should not substitute itself for the initiatives of individuals and voluntary associations.

The principle of subsidiarity became codified in Article 5 of the Treaty on European Union, linking the political architecture of the European social market economy directly to the tradition inaugurated by *Rerum Novarum*.

## Legacy and influence

*Rerum Novarum* has been described as the Magna Carta of Catholic social teaching. It initiated a tradition of papal social encyclicals that continues to the present:

- *Quadragesimo anno* (Pius XI, 1931) — fortieth anniversary; coined "subsidiarity"; addressed the social problem in the context of the Great Depression and the rise of totalitarianism
- *Mater et Magistra* (John XXIII, 1961) — seventieth anniversary; addressed development, international inequality, and socialization
- *Pacem in Terris* (John XXIII, 1963) — addressed human rights in the international order
- *Populorum Progressio* (Paul VI, 1967) — development economics from a Catholic perspective
- *Laborem Exercens* (John Paul II, 1981) — ninetieth anniversary; work as the key to the social question; priority of labour over capital
- *Centesimus Annus* (John Paul II, 1991) — centenary; addressed collapse of communism and global capitalism
- *Laudato si'* (Francis, 2015) — extended the tradition to the ecological question

The intellectual lineage that *Rerum Novarum* initiated runs directly through distributism (Belloc, Chesterton), the cooperative movement (particularly Mondragón), Christian Democracy (CDU, DC, MRP), and the social market economy (Erhard, Müller-Armack). Each of these traditions explicitly claimed *Rerum Novarum* as foundational authority for a third way between unrestricted capitalism and socialism.
