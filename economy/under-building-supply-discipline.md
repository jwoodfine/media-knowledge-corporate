**Under-building** is the deliberate policy of developing less total net leasable square footage per Development Site than the identified demand in the surrounding Regional Market. Woodfine implements this constraint as a structural feature of the Direct-Hold Solution, not as a response to capital constraints: each Woodfine Building is sized to be fully leased and operating at full occupancy upon completion, rather than sized to capture the maximum leasable area that a given footprint could accommodate. The immediate consequence is full occupancy and waiting list generation. The long-term consequence is preservation of pricing power across the portfolio.

## The Circular Economy Rationale

Woodfine frames the under-building policy as adherence to the **Circular Economy** — the principle that supply should be managed in proportion to sustained productive use rather than maximized relative to capacity. In commercial real estate, conventional speculative development produces buildings sized to capture the largest leasable area per land parcel or floor area ratio allowance, with occupancy filling over time. Under-building reverses this: the building is sized for immediate full occupancy, and any demand above that level is directed to a waiting list that informs the next development decision.

The Circular Economy framing has two practical implications. First, it limits asset obsolescence risk: a building that is always fully leased at market rate does not accumulate vacancy that depresses asset value or creates pressure to reduce rents to fill space. Second, it produces a demand signal that documents market absorption capacity, providing evidence for the underwriting of subsequent buildings in the same Regional Market.

## Waiting Lists as Portfolio Data

The waiting lists generated by under-building function as a prospective demand register for subsequent development phases in the same Regional Market. A waiting list at a Woodfine Building documents the names, professional categories, and space requirements of organizations seeking space that could not be accommodated in the current building. This register informs the timing, sizing, and tenant mix of the next Woodfine Building in the market.

The waiting list data is produced by the building's fully leased status, which is itself a product of the under-building policy. Buildings that are developed to maximum capacity and then lease up over time produce occupancy ramp data, not documented deferred demand. The distinction matters for financing decisions: a waiting list provides evidence of unmet demand at time of commitment to the next building, while an occupancy ramp provides historical absorption data for a building that was initially partially vacant.

## Pricing Power Preservation

Pricing power in office leasing erodes when landlords accept below-market rents to fill vacancy. A building that is continuously over-supplied relative to occupied demand creates pressure on a landlord to reduce headline rent, extend rent-free periods, or increase tenant improvement allowances to attract the marginal tenant. These concessions establish precedents that constrain renewal rent negotiations across the existing lease roll.

Under-building eliminates the conditions that produce this dynamic. A Woodfine Building that is fully leased has no marginal-tenant problem: the current occupancy generates the income stream without requiring below-market concessions, and the waiting list provides evidence that the next tenant, when a lease rolls, will be available at or above the current market rate.

## Relationship to the Prototype

The under-building policy is structurally linked to the Prototype discipline. If a Development Site cannot accommodate the Fixed Floor Plate geometry of the Woodfine prototype without design modification, the site is bypassed. This constraint prevents the portfolio from accumulating bespoke buildings that each require individually negotiated tenant improvements. The consequence is that total net leasable square footage per Woodfine Building is determined by the Prototype geometry and the site's conforming footprint, not by a site-specific area maximization exercise. The under-building policy operates within this constraint: the building is sized at the Prototype standard for the site, and that standard is applied at a level that produces full occupancy, not maximum leasable area.
